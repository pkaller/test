Why RFQ Beats Open-Market Execution

For institutional options trades, how you execute matters as much as what you trade. Request for Quote consistently delivers better prices, less market impact, and a complete audit record — here's why.

The problem with open-market execution

In a liquid equity options market, a retail trader placing a 5-contract order on a listed exchange typically gets filled quickly near the mid-price. The market is deep, spreads are tight, and the order is too small to move anything. Scale that up to an institutional block — 100, 200, or 500 contracts — and the dynamics change entirely.

When a large order appears in the public order book, every other participant can see it. Market makers, proprietary traders, and algorithms all adjust their quotes in anticipation. The result is adverse selection: the moment you signal your intent, the price moves against you. For a 200-contract block in a moderately liquid crypto option, this effect alone can cost $0.10–$0.40 per contract — thousands of dollars on a single trade, before you've even looked at the spread.

Crypto options compound this problem. Unlike listed equity options with centralised exchange depth, crypto options trade across a fragmented landscape of OTC dealers, structured product desks, and synthetic replication providers. There is no single venue with reliable displayed liquidity for block size. An open-market approach in this environment means either accepting wide spreads from whoever happens to be quoting, or breaking the order into pieces that erode the economics of the whole program.

How RFQ works

A Request for Quote inverts the information dynamic. Instead of broadcasting your order to the market, you send a private, simultaneous request to a curated list of competing market makers — specifying the underlying, strike, expiry, size, and direction. Each dealer has a short window (typically a few seconds) to respond with their best price. They know they are competing, but they don't know what others are quoting.

This structure creates a real-time auction with two powerful properties:

  • No signalling — your order is not visible to the broader market until it's done, eliminating pre-trade adverse selection entirely
  • Forced competition — dealers who want the flow must quote aggressively, driving prices toward theoretical fair value rather than the wide end of their spread
  • Complete documentation — every quote received is time-stamped and retained, providing a contemporaneous audit record of market conditions at execution
  • Consistent coverage — even for less liquid tokens, we ensure all relevant market makers are contacted, not just the most obvious ones

"The RFQ process transforms execution from a passive, price-taking process into an active price discovery mechanism. You are running a miniature competitive auction — and the market makers know it."

The numbers: what spread compression is worth

The economic benefit of RFQ over open-market execution is not theoretical. Across a typical crypto covered call program, the spread compression from competitive quoting averages $0.10–$0.30 per contract on block trades. That may sound modest, but consider the cumulative picture:

$0.10–0.30 Per-contract spread improvement on a typical block trade
3–8 Competing market makers per RFQ, ensuring genuine price competition
100% Of trades documented with full competing quote record for audit

On a program executing 100-contract blocks monthly across a $10M portfolio, that spread compression represents $12,000–$36,000 in annual execution savings — a meaningful contribution to net yield before accounting for the signalling costs avoided. In percentage terms, better execution can add 10–30 basis points to net program returns.

Why this matters more in crypto

In traditional equity options markets, listed exchanges provide centralised liquidity and reasonably tight markets even for mid-sized blocks. In crypto, the options market is structurally different:

  • Liquidity is fragmented across OTC desks, structured product providers, and synthetic replication models
  • Bid-ask spreads for crypto options are typically 5–15x wider than comparable equity options
  • Price discovery requires active outreach — passive limit orders often sit unfilled or fill at the worst available price
  • Smaller tokens may have only 2–3 natural market makers; ensuring all are contacted requires deliberate process, not just routing to the biggest dealer

RFQ is therefore not just an execution refinement in crypto options — it is the only method that reliably accesses competitive pricing across the full dealer landscape. A program that executes via open market or single-dealer quotes is almost certainly leaving yield on the table on every trade.

Fiduciary documentation as a by-product

For foundations and treasuries with governance obligations, the audit trail generated by RFQ has independent value beyond execution quality. Every trade produces a complete record: the full set of competing quotes, the time of each response, the winning quote, and the executed price. This record demonstrates — contemporaneously, not retrospectively — that the best available market price was obtained.

Where investment committees or boards require evidence of best execution, the RFQ log is the standard of proof. No other execution method produces equivalent documentation as a natural by-product of the trade itself.
